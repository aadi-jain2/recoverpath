The clinical problem

1 in 4

heart-failure patients are readmitted to the hospital within 30 days of discharge.

Deterioration is invisible — until it isn't.

Heart failure is the leading cause of hospital readmission in the United States. COPD patients face a similar post-discharge risk. Between hospital visits, fluid buildup, low oxygen, arrhythmia, and worsening cough develop quietly — invisible to the patient until they're back in crisis.

Hospitals feel this too: CMS's Hospital Readmissions Reduction Program penalizes excess readmission rates, so the incentive to catch decline earlier is clinical and financial. RecoverPath exists to close the monitoring gap between discharge and the next crisis.

The RecoverPath solution

One strap. Five signals. Worn where it matters.

RecoverPath is a chest strap for post-discharge, at-home monitoring — built to detect pulmonary edema and respiratory deterioration days before symptoms are noticeable, giving clinicians time to intervene before a hospital visit is needed.

The chest is the most direct, stable place to read thoracic fluid, heart electrical activity, and lung sound together — one wearable location instead of several, which matters for real-world adherence after discharge. It's the only wearable combining bioimpedance, ECG, SpO2, acoustic sensing, and activity in a single strap.

  • 01Detect fluid buildup days before shortness of breath sets in.
  • 02Flag new arrhythmia and dropping oxygen while the patient is still at home.
  • 03Surface worsening cough and falling activity as early functional-decline signals.
  • 04Route meaningful changes — not raw data — to the care team that can act on them.

The flagship signal

Fluid in the lungs, measured before it's felt.

Pulmonary edema is the direct cause of most heart-failure readmissions — and it's measurable days before a patient feels short of breath. Thoracic bioimpedance sends a tiny, imperceptible electrical signal across the chest and measures how easily it passes through tissue: fluid conducts electricity differently than healthy lung tissue, so the trend line reveals buildup before symptoms do.

Very few consumer or wearable devices attempt this. CardioMEMS is the closest existing comparator — but it's an invasive, surgically implanted pulmonary-artery sensor. RecoverPath aims for a comparable early-warning signal non-invasively, from a chest strap.

Technology & prototype

What's actually inside the strap.

An ESP32-C6 microcontroller integrates the full sensor stack inside a 3D-printed chest-piece housing, worn on an adjustable chest band. On-device preprocessing and risk scoring flag meaningful changes — not raw data dumps — before anything is transmitted.

Clinical workflow

From discharge to avoided readmission.

Five stages connect a patient leaving the hospital to a care team acting early enough to keep them out of it.

01

Discharge

Patient is fitted with the RecoverPath chest strap before leaving the hospital.

02

Home monitoring

The strap continuously tracks fluid, rhythm, oxygen, respiratory sound, and activity.

03

Early detection

On-device and backend analysis flags concerning trends — rising fluid, new arrhythmia, dropping SpO2.

04

Clinician outreach

Care teams are alerted and can intervene — medication adjustment, a check-in call, or an early visit.

05

Outcome

Deterioration is caught and treated at home, before it becomes a hospital readmission.

Evidence & roadmap

The reasoning behind the sensor stack.

  • Bioimpedance as a fluid signal

    Thoracic impedance trends have long been studied as a non-invasive proxy for pulmonary fluid status, tracking changes days ahead of clinically reported symptoms.

    Fluid-status monitoring literature
  • AFib and decompensation

    Atrial fibrillation is a well-documented trigger for acute heart-failure decompensation, implicated in roughly a quarter to a third of episodes.

    Arrhythmia & HF outcomes literature
  • Acoustic sensing for COPD

    Cough and wheeze acoustics are an established, FDA-cleared basis for respiratory monitoring in comparable products, supporting the case for sound as a standalone biomarker.

    Respiratory acoustic monitoring literature
Current stage

Early prototyping and bench testing on simulated signals — with early pilot interest from hospital partners for future discharge-monitoring programs.

  1. Signal validation against reference measurements.
  2. Algorithm tuning on real-world data.
  3. IRB approval for a clinical pilot study.
  4. Regulatory strategy scoping (FDA 510(k) pathway).

Along the way, informal conversations with physicians and researchers in pulmonology, sleep medicine, and critical-care AI have helped sanity-check the reasoning behind the sensor stack and use cases.

Team & story

Built by people who kept asking "why isn't this solved yet?"

We kept coming back to the same gap: a patient leaves the hospital feeling fine, and by the time anyone — including them — notices something's wrong, it's an ER visit again. A wrist wearable couldn't read fluid or lung sound. A patch couldn't cover rhythm and breathing together. The chest was the only place all of it lived in one signal.

RecoverPath is a student-built medtech project from a multidisciplinary team spanning biotech, AI, hardware, and clinical/business research. We're early — bench-testing on simulated signals, not yet a finished product — but the reasoning behind every sensor on this strap has been argued over, prototyped, and rebuilt more than once.
